# Superheat → Distributed Compute Network + Asset-Origination Engine

**A one-pager on transitioning Superheat from a hardware company into a compute-asset *manufacturer* — originating contracted cash flows, packaging them, and selling them to institutional investors to recycle capital.**

*v0.1 — internal working draft, June 2026*

---

## 0. The thesis in one sentence

Superheat's product was never the water heater, and it isn't even the compute. The real product is a **20-million-node distributed data center that finances its own growth** — each heater is an asset that *originates a contracted compute cash flow*, and the high-margin business is **creating, packaging, and distributing those cash flows to yield-hungry capital**, exactly the way Carvana industrialized auto-loan origination and Sunrun turned solar leases into securities.

> Dealerships sell cars and outsource financing. Carvana industrialized loan origination + ABS. Solar companies turned 25-year electricity payments + tax credits into financial assets.
> **Superheat turns the installed base of heaters into the world's largest untapped compute substrate — and turns the compute revenue into a securitizable asset class.**

## 2. The reframe: a heater is an originated asset

A Superheat unit is structurally identical to a **solar lease** or a **financed auto**: a one-time hardware placement that throws off a long-dated, contracted, relatively predictable cash flow.

```
Regular heater:     Electricity  →  Hot water                       (cost center)
Superheat unit:     Electricity  →  Hot water  +  ~$34K/yr compute   (revenue-generating asset)
```

Each installed H1 is, in financial terms:

- **Asset life:** ~10 years (matches heater lifespan in the deck)
- **Gross cash flow:** ~$34K/yr compute value
- **Net spread:** compute value − embedded electricity (~$4.9K/yr, already paid as the heating bill) − servicing/opex
- **Collateral:** the physical unit + the host site's standing heating demand (the load is *needed*, not speculative)

Once you see the fleet as **a pool of ~10-year contracted cash flows**, the entire securitization playbook from autos and solar maps directly onto it.

## 3. The playbook, ported from autos & solar

> **Originate → package → sell → recycle capital → repeat.**

| Stage | Auto / Carvana | Solar (Sunrun/Sunnova) | **Superheat** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Originate** | Write the auto loan | Sign the 20–25 yr lease/PPA | **Install the heater = originate a ~10 yr compute-revenue contract** |
| **2. Season** | Collect a few months of payments | Energize + meter production | **Run nodes, meter uptime + compute delivered, build a track record** |
| **3. Package** | Bundle thousands of loans → ABS | Pool thousands of leases → ABS | **Pool thousands of compute nodes → "Compute-Backed Securities" (CBS)** |
| **4. Distribute** | Sell tranches to institutions | Sell tranches to institutions | **Sell senior/mezz tranches to insurers, pensions, credit funds** |
| **5. Recycle** | Proceeds fund new loans | Proceeds fund new installs | **Proceeds fund the next 10,000 heaters — growth without dilutive equity** |
| **6. Service** | Keep servicing rights (0.5–2%/yr) | Keep O&M + servicing | **Keep the orchestration layer + servicing fee on every node, forever** |

The machine becomes self-funding: **every dollar of CBS sold buys the hardware for the next cohort of nodes**, while Superheat keeps origination economics, servicing fees, and the residual.

## 4. The four revenue layers (where the money actually is)

The least attractive part is the physical box. Mirroring the reference, value concentrates in the financial layer:

1. **Origination margin** — highest ROE. The spread between deploying a node and the discounted value of its contracted compute stream. (Carvana's "gain-on-sale" analog — see §6 risk.)
2. **Structuring** — bundling nodes into rated tranches. Fees accrue to whoever structures (initially a bank partner; later in-house).
3. **Distribution** — steady institutional yield. Investors buy the right to future compute payments; Superheat converts 10 years of future cash into capital *today*.
4. **Servicing / orchestration** — the sticky, recurring layer. Even after a node's cash flow is sold, Superheat still routes the workloads, guarantees uptime, and clips a servicing fee (target **0.5–2%/yr of node value**). This is the moat: investors own the cash flows, but **only Superheat can operate the fleet.**

```
Physical heater        → commodity, near-zero margin
Compute orchestration  → recurring, sticky, high margin
Asset origination      → highest ROE
Securitization/servicing → recurring fee annuity
```

## 5. Why Superheat's asset is *better* collateral than auto or solar ABS

This is the pitch to capital markets — and it is genuinely differentiated:

- **Demand is non-discretionary.** People heat water whether or not AI is in fashion. The electricity is an *embedded heating expenditure*, not an incremental compute cost — so the marginal cost of the compute is effectively **zero** (deck's core economic claim). Margins survive a compute-price downturn that would wipe out a pure data-center borrower.
- **Near-1.0 PUE = structurally lower opex** than any centralized competitor in the pool's underlying assets. Lower opex → fatter, more defensible excess spread → safer senior tranches.
- **Geographic + grid diversification.** 100M nodes across millions of buildings is a naturally diversified pool — no single-site grid, permitting, or natural-disaster concentration. The opposite of a $2B single-campus data-center loan.
- **Dual collateral.** If compute economics soften, the unit is *still a working water heater* with standalone resale/utility value. The downside asset is real, not stranded silicon.
- **Hardware-light scaling.** Nodes deploy "room by room," incrementally, with near-zero incremental building cost — so the pool can grow continuously to feed continuous issuance, rather than in lumpy multi-year data-center build cycles.

## 6. The tax-equity layer (the solar lesson)

Solar's *most* profitable layer was often **not** the electricity — it was monetizing the **30% federal ITC** by selling tax benefits to banks/corporates with large tax bills. Superheat should explicitly hunt for the equivalent:

- **Energy-efficiency / electrification incentives** on high-efficiency water heating and heat-pump-class appliances (utility rebates, demand-response payments, efficiency credits).
- **Demand-response & grid-services revenue** — a distributed, dispatchable, interruptible load is a *grid asset*; utilities pay for that flexibility. This is a second contracted cash flow stacked on the same node.
- **Accelerated depreciation** on the deployed hardware base, packaged into tax-equity partnerships for counterparties who need the shield.

Each incentive that can be **monetized up front** improves origination economics and shortens the already-fast 9.8-month payback.

## 7. The honest risk section (the 2008 / Carvana warning)

The reference flagged the danger directly, and it applies here — **name it before investors do:**

- **Issuance-market dependence.** The flywheel assumes investors keep buying CBS. If structured-credit markets freeze, loan/CBS sales stop → Superheat must fund hardware off its own balance sheet → liquidity crunch. *Mitigation:* keep a balance-sheet warehouse line; never let growth outrun committed forward-flow agreements.
- **Gain-on-sale optics.** Recognizing future compute profit *today* (Carvana's magic, and its biggest credibility problem) flatters earnings and front-loads risk. *Mitigation:* disclose residual assumptions transparently; prefer conservative seasoning before sale.
- **Compute-price risk.** The ~$34K/yr rests on a benchmark GPU-hour price ($0.49) that can fall. *Mitigation:* the non-discretionary-heating floor (§5) caps the downside; underwrite tranches to a stressed compute price, not spot.
- **Servicing concentration.** Investors must trust that Superheat can keep 100M nodes running. *Mitigation:* the orchestration moat is also the servicing guarantee — make it auditable.

## 8. The transition in three phases

**Phase 1 — Prove the asset (now → ~12 mo).**
Standardize node-level metering and reporting. Produce a clean, audited track record of per-unit compute cash flow and uptime across the 1,000+ live units. Goal: a credit file an underwriter can rate. *Outcome: the asset becomes legible.*

**Phase 2 — First warehouse + forward flow (~12–24 mo).**
Secure a warehouse facility to fund hardware against contracted cash flows. Sign forward-flow agreements with 1–2 institutional buyers (insurer / credit fund). Stack the first demand-response and incentive revenues onto nodes. *Outcome: growth funded by debt, not dilution.*

**Phase 3 — Programmatic securitization (~24 mo+).**
Inaugural rated CBS issuance. Retain servicing + residual. Recycle proceeds into the next cohorts, scaling toward the 100M-node / 2030 target as a self-financing engine. *Outcome: Superheat is a compute-asset manufacturer with a recurring fee annuity.*

Traditional data centers raise capital, build a single concentrated campus, and burn 40–90% of their energy on cooling; **Superheat instead originates millions of distributed, dual-use compute nodes whose energy is already paid for as heating — then industrializes the origination, packaging, and distribution of those contracted compute cash flows into a new institutional asset class, recycling the capital to grow the network without dilution.** The heater is the commodity; **the manufactured, securitized compute cash flow is the business.**
