The Thesis

The Product Isn’t the Heater.
It Isn’t Even the Compute.

Every great financing business runs the same loop. Dealerships run it on cars. Carvana industrialized it. Solar turned 25 years of electricity payments into an asset class.

Originate→ Package→ Sell→ Recycle capital→ Repeat

Superheat runs it on heat: every installed unit originates a contracted compute cash flow. The high-margin business is manufacturing, packaging, and distributing that cash flow — and the network finances its own growth.

Where the Money Actually Is

Four Layers.
The Box Is the Cheapest One.

Layer 1 · Commodity
Physical hardware

The heater, the install labor. Near-zero margin at scale — the least attractive part of the business. It exists to create the other three layers.

Layer 2 · Highest ROE
Asset origination

The spread between deploying a node and the discounted value of its contracted compute stream — Carvana’s gain-on-sale analog, recognized conservatively (see pre-mortem).

Layer 3 · Conversion
Structuring & distribution

Bundle nodes into rated tranches; sell ten years of future compute cash for capital today. Fees accrue to whoever structures — a bank partner first, in-house later.

Layer 4 · The Moat
Servicing & orchestration

Sticky, recurring, never sold. Investors can own the cash flows, but only Superheat can operate the fleet — routing workloads, guaranteeing uptime, clipping 0.5–2%/yr of node value, forever.

The Physics of the Margin

Has Hours.

Electricity is only free while the host actually wants heat — it’s an embedded heating bill, not a compute cost. A family home’s hot water absorbs ~16% of the unit’s 28,900 kWh/yr thermal output. A hotel absorbs nearly all of it.

The Pitch to Capital Markets

Better Collateral Than
Solar or Auto Paper.

Demand is non-discretionary

People heat water whether or not AI is in fashion. Margins survive a compute-price downturn that would wipe out a pure data-center borrower.

Structurally lower opex

Near-1.0 PUE — no cooling bill in the pool’s underlying assets. Lower opex → fatter excess spread → safer senior tranches.

Naturally diversified

Thousands of nodes across thousands of buildings and grids. The opposite of a $2B single-campus data-center loan.

Dual collateral

If compute economics soften, the unit is still a working water heater with standalone value. The downside asset is real, not stranded silicon.

Hardware-light scaling

Nodes deploy room by room, continuously — feeding continuous issuance instead of lumpy multi-year build cycles.

It pays the borrower

Solar loans only cost the homeowner. The H1’s income offsets the payment — a credit story no other home-improvement asset has.

Financing Architecture

Two Assets.
Two Machines.

Product A
Residential → Loan Machine

Customer owns the unit. $6–9K installed, financed ~12.5% APR × 10 yr ≈ $88–132/mo. The compute share offsets the payment — up to fully. Superheat keeps hardware margin + origination + servicing; the loan lives on someone else’s balance sheet.

Template: GoodLeap home-improvement ABS. Water heaters are already an eligible asset class.

Product B
Commercial → Yield Machine

Superheat owns the unit (third-party ownership). Host signs a 10-yr heat-services agreement: discounted heat, minimum draw, space + meter. Units sit in a bankruptcy-remote SPV; compute revenue + heat fees fund asset-backed notes.

Template: Sunrun lease structure × CoreWeave GPU collateral package.

One orchestration & servicing layer underneath both. It is never sold.

Rating Fuel · The Solar Tax-Equity Lesson

Manufacture
Contracted Dollars.

Solar’s most profitable layer was never the electricity — it was monetizing incentives and contracts. Every contracted GPU-hour and hedged dollar moves the rating up and the coupon down.

Demand response / VPP

$20–100 per unit-year of utility-grade income. EnergyHub already aggregates water heaters for 30+ utilities — the integration is pre-built for our device class. DOE prices VPP capacity at ~half a gas peaker. At 100K units: $2–10M/yr contracted.

Reserved compute

1/3/6-month committed terms convert spot exposure into contract. SF Compute’s forward market can sell 12–24-month strips on a fleet slice. Covenant: ≥30% of fleet GPU-hours contracted by deal close.

Heat-services agreements

Minimum-draw clauses are contracted commodity sales — heat-as-a-service, the direct analog of a solar PPA, on every commercial unit.

Incentives at origination

Efficiency rebates, electrification programs, accelerated depreciation packaged into tax-equity partnerships, Luxor hashprice forwards on legacy ASIC units — monetized up front, GoodLeap-style, to compress the CAPEX basis.

Execution

Four Phases to a
Self-Funding Machine.

Phase 1 · now → 12 mo
Build the tape

1–2K commercial units ($18–36M, equity + venture debt). Meter everything: GPU-hours, realized $/hr, utilization, uptime, thermal draw. Residential launches through a partner originator at zero balance-sheet cost. Exit: 12 months of clean tape on ≥1,000 units, ≥30% contracted revenue.

Phase 2 · 12–24 mo
Warehouse + forward flow

$25–50M revolving facility against the seasoned fleet (~70–75% advance); residential forward-flow commitments signed; KBRA/Moody’s pre-engaged; fleet-wide VPP enrollment. Exit: warehouse ≥70% utilized, DSCR ≥2.5× trailing six months.

Phase 3 · 24–36 mo
Inaugural ABS

$150M Trust 2027-1 as specified. Proceeds recycle into the next 8–10K units. Servicing strip and residual stay home. Exit: priced inside S+300 blended, advance ≥75%.

Phase 4 · 36 mo+
Programmatic

2–4 deals/yr, residential and commercial shelves separate, IG senior classes on the CoreWeave precedent. At 100K units: ~$1.2B/yr collections supporting ~$1.4B of standing ABS — growth compounds on other people’s balance sheets.

This Stage Doesn’t Need VCs.
It Needs Asset People.
