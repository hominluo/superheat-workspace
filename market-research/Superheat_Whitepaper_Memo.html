0Executive summary

Superheat’s product was never the water heater, and it is not even the compute. Each installed H1 converts a standing heating bill into a metered compute cash flow, and the durable, high-margin business is originating, packaging, and distributing those cash flows to institutional capital — then recycling the proceeds into the next cohort of units, so the network finances its own growth.

This memorandum re-underwrites the business at live June-2026 GPU-market prices and reaches three conclusions. First, at honest utilization a commercial unit nets roughly $12K per year — about one-third of the v3.5 marketing headline, and for the first time underwritable: an 18-month payback, ~45% unlevered IRR, and 2.0× debt-service coverage under full stress. Second, the correct corporate strategy is to manufacture two financial assets — prime home-improvement loans (residential) and contracted thermal-compute cash flows (commercial) — funded through the now-proven GPU-collateral, solar-lease, and home-improvement ABS channels. Third, the counterparties for every stage of that machine exist today and are actively writing comparable deals; this document names them and the specific ask for each.

The loop, ported from autos and solar: originate → season → package → distribute → recycle capital → repeat — while Superheat permanently retains origination fees, the 0.5–2%/yr servicing strip, and the residual. The edge over every predecessor: solar collateral only costs the homeowner money; a Superheat unit pays them.

A Superheat unit is structurally identical to a solar lease or a financed auto: a one-time hardware placement that throws off a long-dated, contracted, relatively predictable cash flow. A regular heater turns electricity into hot water — a cost center. An H1 turns the same electricity into hot water plus compute revenue — an income-producing asset with two cash legs (variable compute rental income and fixed contracted heat-service fees) and dual collateral: if compute economics soften, the unit is still a working water heater serving a standing heat demand. Once the fleet is seen as a pool of ten-year cash flows, the entire securitization playbook from autos and solar maps directly onto it.

The single most important underwriting input is the price-decline curve. The v3.5 assumption of $0.49/hr flat for ten years is not financeable; a declining curve with fast amortization is. Every number that follows bakes it in.

3.3 — The thermal constraint (the physics of the margin)

Electricity is only “free” while the host actually wants heat — it is an embedded heating bill, not an incremental compute cost. A single-family home’s hot-water load (~4,500 kWh/yr) absorbs only ~16% of a unit’s 28,900 kWh/yr maximum thermal output; a cold-climate home with hydronic space heating absorbs ~45%; hotels, apartment buildings, and campuses absorb 85%+ on near-continuous draw — that is the securitizable asset class. Compute can still run outside heat-demand hours at retail power (~$0.07/GPU-hr cost against a ~$0.30 net rate — thinner margin, never negative when rented), and the tank itself is a thermal battery: scheduling shifts heating into rented hours.

4The financing architecture: two assets, two machines

The business splits along the same line the consumer-finance market already splits, with one orchestration and servicing layer underneath both that is never sold.

Product A — Residential: the consumer-loan origination machine

The customer owns the unit: $6–9K installed, financed at ~12.5% APR over 10 years ($88–132/month), with the customer’s share of compute income (~50% of net) running $96–350/month — payment offset up to full coverage; in cold-climate hydronic homes the heater effectively pays its own loan. The template is GoodLeap’s home-improvement ABS shelf, where water heaters are already an eligible asset class: GoodLeap 2026-1 securitized 37,987 loans ($11,960 average, 12.56% WAC, 757 weighted-average FICO), raising $408.9M with 21.9% Class-A enhancement. An H1 loan is the same paper — prime homeowner, UCC-1 on the fixture — plus an income-producing asset behind it: strictly better credit than a window or HVAC loan. Execution sequence: partner-originate (zero balance-sheet risk) → captive origination → forward-flow whole loans at par → own shelf past $200M/yr of originations. Superheat economics per placement: $2.8–5.3K (hardware margin $2–4K, origination fee 2–5%, dealer spread, servicing-strip NPV), mostly cash up front, while the loan lives on someone else’s balance sheet.

Product B — Commercial TPO: the securitizable core

Superheat owns the unit (third-party ownership). The host signs a 10-year Heat Services Agreement — hot water at 10–20% below grid-equivalent cost, a guaranteed minimum thermal draw, space and metering. Units sit in a bankruptcy-remote AssetCo SPV pledging equipment (UCC-1), HSA contracts, compute receivables, platform accounts, and SPV equity. Each unit carries two cash legs: variable compute revenue (~$12K/yr base) and fixed contracted heat fees ($40–80/month) plus demand-response income. The fixed leg anchors the rating; the compute leg is the excess spread. Debt sizing: 75% advance on $18K = $13.5K per unit; five-year amortizing at ~9% = $3.47K/yr of debt service → DSCR 3.5× base, 2.0× under full stress (50% utilization, −30% price). The collateral precedent is established: CoreWeave’s GPU-backed facilities progressed $2.3B (2023) → $7.5B (2024, Blackstone/Magnetar) → an $8.5B DDTL rated A3 at SOFR+225 (2026) — GPUs in an SPV clear institutional credit committees.

Product C — Host equipment financing (optional accelerant)

Vast.ai already operates “Vast Finance,” matching GPU hosts with equipment-collateral leases and revenue-based loans underwritten on verified platform earnings. Superheat can replicate this for franchised installers and building owners who want to own units — originating a third securitizable receivable off its own balance sheet. Dell and HPE Financial Services’ as-a-service GPU programs offer a parallel lever: leasing the compute modules shifts CAPEX off the SPV and simplifies the year-5 refresh covenant.

Enhancement stack and covenants (benchmarked to Sunrun Pangea/Bacchus and Sunnova Sol VI): overcollateralization ~20% (Sunrun comp: 19.5%); six-month senior-interest liquidity reserve; a funded GPU-refresh reserve — the analog of solar’s inverter-replacement reserve and the deal’s novel feature — covering the year-5 module swap; 5–6-year amortization matched to GPU revenue life, not solar’s 25; turbo amortization on three-month-average DSCR < 1.5×; cash trap on fleet utilization < 40% or single-platform concentration > 60%; minimum metered-uptime SLA per unit; named backup servicer with escrowed orchestration runbooks.

Rating calibration (verified against the June-2026 comp set). Single-A senior is the market print for this collateral family, not conservatism: GoodLeap 2026-1 priced A−/BBB/BB−; Sunrun’s 2026 deal printed A− seniors (its record 2024 deal reached A+, sixteen securitizations in); Vantage data-center ABS prints A−; CoreWeave’s GPU facility is rated A3. AA/AAA exists only for seasoned paper — Mosaic’s AAA came on upgrade with ~50% enhancement, and Switch’s AAA reflects twenty years of operating history. For a first-of-a-kind issuer, A/A− is the ambitious end of realistic and BBB+ is the quiet downside case; the structure works at either, since the base DSCR of 3.5× absorbs the ~40–60 bps coupon difference.

Market readiness. The rulebook now exists — none of it did two years ago: KBRA published data-center ABS criteria (Jan 2026) and already rates the GoodLeap shelf; Moody’s (Feb 2025), S&P (2024, revised 2025), and Fitch (2025 drafts) followed. Roughly $57B of US data-center securitization has priced since 2021 (~$26B in 2025 alone; $30–40B/yr projected through 2027) — and every deal in that market is single-campus risk. A 10,000-site distributed pool is a portfolio-construction gift to a starved buyer base. Plan: engage KBRA plus one of Moody’s/S&P at warehouse stage with the metered tape; target a rated deal within 12 months of warehouse close.

6Manufacturing contracted revenue (rating fuel)

Solar’s most profitable layer was never the electricity — it was monetizing incentives and contracts. Every contracted GPU-hour and hedged dollar moves the rating up and the coupon down — the difference between BB paper and A paper.

  • Demand response / VPP enrollment — $20–100 per unit-year of utility-grade income. EnergyHub already aggregates Rheem water heaters for 30+ utilities (the integration is pre-built for this device class); DOE prices VPP capacity at roughly half a gas peaker. At 100K units: $2–10M/yr of contracted income.
  • Reserved-instance compute — 1/3/6-month committed terms convert spot exposure into contract; SF Compute’s forward market can sell 12–24-month strips on a fleet slice. Covenant target: ≥30% of fleet GPU-hours contracted by deal close.
  • Heat-services agreements — the HSA minimum-draw clause is itself a contracted commodity sale: heat-as-a-service, the direct analog of a solar PPA, on every commercial unit.
  • Incentives monetized at origination — efficiency rebates, electrification programs, accelerated depreciation packaged into tax-equity partnerships, and Luxor hashprice forwards on legacy ASIC units — taken up front, GoodLeap-style, to compress the CAPEX basis.

Why the dead died — and why this structure won’t

  • Monoline ABS dependence (Mosaic ’25 · Sunlight ’23 · Sunnova ’25 — all Chapter 11) — one revenue leg, assumed take-out. Counter: hardware margin, servicing, and compute are independent legs; growth gated by committed capital, never assumed take-out.
  • Hidden dealer fees (MN AG v. GoodLeap/Sunlight/Mosaic/Dividend — $35M). Counter: a published, transparent dealer fee; income-producing collateral removes the incentive to stuff fees.
  • Duration mismatch (Mosaic’s failed refinancing) — 25-year paper funded short into a rate spike. Counter: 5–6-year amortizing structures matched to GPU life.
  • Collateral that only costs the borrower (every solar-loan book). Counter: the H1 pays the borrower.

10Conclusion

Traditional data centers raise capital, build a single concentrated campus, and burn 40–90% of their energy on cooling. Superheat instead originates millions of distributed, dual-use compute nodes whose energy is already paid for as heating — then industrializes the origination, packaging, and distribution of those contracted cash flows into a new institutional asset class, recycling the capital to grow the network without dilution.

The heater is the commodity. The manufactured cash flow is the business.

At live June-2026 prices and honest utilization, the per-unit numbers are roughly one-third of the v3.5 headline — and they are finally the kind of numbers an underwriter can sign: ~$12K net per commercial unit-year, an 18-month payback, ~45% unlevered IRR, and twice-covered debt service under full stress. The collateral has working comparables in three proven ABS channels, the rating methodologies were published within the last eighteen months, and the specific counterparties for every rung of the funding ladder are writing checks into adjacent deals this quarter. What remains is execution: build the metered tape, gate growth on committed capital, and never sell the servicing layer.
